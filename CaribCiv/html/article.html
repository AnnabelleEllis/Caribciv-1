Caribbean Integration Network

“Achieving regional integration one step at a time”

In today’s world of globalization, do efforts at Caribbean integration still have any relevance for Caribbean development, or should we all aspire to be citizens of the global village?

CIN Reporter,

Annabelle Ellis

April 5,2019

Globalization, also defined as the increased interconnectedness and interdependence of peoples and countries, is generally understood to include two inter-related elements: the opening of international borders to increasingly fast flows of goods, services, finance, people and ideas; and the changes in institutions and policies at national and international levels that facilitate or promote such flows. (WHO) World health organization

Regional integration is the process by which two or more nation-states agree to co-operate and work closely together to achieve peace, stability and wealth. Usually integration involves one or more written agreements that describe the areas of cooperation in detail, as well as some coordinating bodies representing the countries involved. This co-operation usually begins with economic integration and as it continues, comes to include political integration. Regional Integration in the Caribbean is mainly through the Caribbean Community, or CARICOM. In addition, member states in the sub-region are members of the Organization of the Eastern Caribbean States (OECS).

The global village is described as the world viewed as a community in which distance and isolation have been dramatically reduced by electronic media (such as television and the Internet)

The Caribbean is a region that has been invaded by European countries which lead to an ill-management of resources. This subsequently had negative impacts on Caribbean development because the assets in the Caribbean region were not utilized properly. Caribbean economies became distorted and success was geared towards the elite instead of the Caribbean peoples. Thus, there was economic growth but negative development among he Caribbean peoples. As stated best by Coleman and Nixson, “it is possible to have economic growth with negative development in that, although average incomes may have risen , the economic lot of the mass of the population would have deteriorated and negative or no progress would have been made in transforming personal attitudes and institutions in the manner required by modernization ideals”. This has led to inequalities in income distribution and unequal access to education, primary health care, financial credit and technology. This kind of development is a dependent rather than an autonomous one.

In dependent development, there is unwanted large scale interference in the political, social and economic aspect of a country. In relation to the Caribbean, the transnational co-corporations (TNC’s) such as Nestle, Colgate Palmolive, Johnson and Johnson etc. came to enhance their profitability. These companies provide little to no meaningful economic activity in the region as they are involved either through a parent or subsidiary company. However, autonomous development is where there is freedom from unwanted interference, the freedom of trade, the accumulation and utilization of domestic savings, and strong indigenous management. Cuba is great example of autonomous development. However, there are some step backs in implementing this kind of development, Cuba has felt the repercussions of this. They have faced being isolated from trade and being denied access to lending institutions such as the IMF and the World Bank. This is what the region fears if this decision is chosen. This is so because the region does not have the necessary local capital to fund the kind of development yearned for in the Caribbean.

Countries taking loans from the financial institutions such as the IMF has no bargaining power in the structural adjustments if they would like to secure the loan desired. Thus these countries find that they have no control over their economic affairs since the IMF determines the national economic priorities and goals. The IMF policies usually involve severe regimen of financial cuts in government expenditure, an end to subsidies on a wide range of basic foods for the poor and stringent taxation. IMF demands a laying off of a large number of public servants and a policy of wage restraint for both public and private sector, a severe reduction in welfare programmes, including unemployment maternity and sickness benefits etc. An example of this can be seen with Dominican Republic, Jamaica and Guyana. These countries have found it hard to make short term structural adjustments and have experienced severe struggles to achieve economic growth. As a result of this the Caribbean has been dependent on the developed countries. It has been a never ending cycle of being slave to countries that have much more power.

The Caribbean has also been attacked with the issue of globalization through technology where more geographical borders disintegrate and the Caribbean culture is being erased and replaced by other cultures in different spheres of the world. Thus, the Caribbean is being bombarded with the effects of globalization with no means of escaping.

In order to combat this issue of the Caribbean buried in effects of globalization, the region has developed CARICOM, CDB and many more. CARICOM was established under the treaty of Chaguaramas in 1973. Though notcreated for political purposes CARICOM has the opportunity to create political union between member states. Political integration has its advantages however itruns the risk of political federation under the guise of CARICOM. WithinCARICOM there are sub unions working towards the goal of integration such asCaribbean Single Market and Economy and the Caribbean Court of Justice.Previously well established unions such as the Free Trade Area of the Americas(FTAA) and the European Union (EU) serves as evidence that Caribbeanintegration is a step in the right direction towards the development of the region.CSME gives a major boost to the economy where it creates a larger market forbuyers and sellers of the region and money circulating within the region certainly attributes to development. The CSME is more than just a common a market, itsspeaks to a unified economy which can attract foreign investors and also giveregional entrepreneurs an international market. There exists unions that are against integration however it can be seen where bigger strides can be made towards thedevelopment of the region when the islands are in unity.
